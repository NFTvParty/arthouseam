Episode 002

The music hip-hop sampled didn’t disappear. It just waited to be reintroduced.

DJRaw Q
AiredApril 10, 2026  ·  9PM EST
StationWPAT 930 AM  ·  New York
FormatOpen format  ·  45s

Liner Notes

The first thing I put on was Parliament. That wasn’t an accident.

Before hip-hop had a name, it had a sample library — and a lot of that library starts with George Clinton. The Mothership wasn’t just a concept album. It was a blueprint. You can trace a direct line from Mothership Connection to almost anything that followed, which is another way of saying: if you understand Parliament, you understand a lot about why hip-hop sounds the way it sounds.

De La Soul shows up four times in this set. I didn’t plan that. It happened because when I dug into the crates, De La kept being the answer. They had a way of making records feel like a conversation — between eras, between artists, between people who might never share a room. That’s what I was going for across these two hours.

The back half goes deep into the source material: Soul Searchers, Manzel, Asha Puthli, Maceo Parker. These are the records hip-hop producers were reaching for in the dark, at the bottom of dollar bins in stores that don’t exist anymore. I wanted to give them their proper moment without the sample attached.

Al Hirt was the wild card. If you know, you know.

This is the music I grew up on and keep coming back to. It doesn’t age because it was never trying to be current. It was trying to be true.

— Raw Q
